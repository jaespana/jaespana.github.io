What is Ozone?

Ozone is an oxygen compound consisting of three oxygen molecules. It is a more toxic and reactive form of oxygen discovered in 1839 by Christian Friedrich Schnbein (Christie, 2001). Due to this nature, Ozone is a very important part of our Earth that must be protected.

What about the Ozone Layer?

Image from Wikimedia Commons.

Our earth consists of several different atmospheric layers. Within one of these layers, the stratosphere, lies the Ozone Layer. This layer, first discovered by Marie Alfred Cornu in 1879, ranges from an altitude of 15-50 kilometers (Christie, 2001). It is quite an important part of the Earths natural systems since, without the ozone layer, our Earth would be way too hot to live in!

Why is the Ozone Layer significant?

To simply put it, the Ozone Layer allows life on Earth. Without it, there would be no life.

Image from PixaBay.

The Ozone molecule has several bands of absorption that absorbs most ultraviolet rays from the sun (Jabbar, 1993). Since most existing Ozone molecules reside in the Ozone Layer, it's bound to be an effective shield which in turn protects and preserves Earths ecosystem.

Uncovering the Problem

While our Ozone Layer is a strong shield that prevents extra ultraviolet rays from piercing the atmosphere, it is also quite fragile and delicate. (One of the reasons being that it's highly reactive, as mentioned before!)

*Note: For the definition regarding the Ozone and Ozone Layer click on the Definition button.

Ozone Reactivity

Ozone molecules break down when they absorb ultraviolet radiation, but are also created when those same molecules combine with other oxygen molecules in the atmosphere(Jabbar, 1993). This creates a state of equilibrium where the speed of decomposition is as fast as the creation of these ozone molecules. However, in recent times, the decomposition rates have been offset by other unnatural processes.

The Cause of Increased Ozone Depletion

These additional factors causing ozone depletion are manmade, and range from agriculture to industrial processes.

Image from PxHere.

A specific example would be Chlorofluorocarbons (or CFCs) which are gases used for refrigeration. These CFCs interact with ozone and release free chlorine atoms that join with other free oxygen atoms (Jabbar, 1993). This prevents the ozone in the ozone layer from replenishing itself! Not only that, since they also interact with ultraviolet radiation, they trap additional heat in the atmosphere.

Types of Greenhouse Gases

These are known as greenhouses gases that cause the greenhouse effect. Several other gases also exist that can interact with Ozone in a negative manner, like carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and more. Carbon dioxide comes from industrial processes involving coal, natural gas, waste, and deforestation while methane comes from agricultural processes involving livestock and the decay of organic waste (Epa.gov, 2020).

Natural 'Fix' for Carbon Emissions

Carbon emissions are part of the natural carbon cycle in our Earth. However, this does not include manmade emissions!

Image from PixaBay.

Plants remove carbon from the atmosphere by absorbing it through their biological process called photosynthesis (Epa.gov, 2020). This mitigates and balances out natural carbon emissions, but, as stated before, does not account for the increased carbon concentrations due to greenhouse gas emissions done by humans.

What Does This Mean?

There is more Ozone being broken down in our atmosphere than that of which is being generated. This leads to the existence of Ozone Holes, something quite bad for our planet Earth!

Ozone Hole Phenomena

The concept of an Ozone Hole was first discovered after vast usage of chlorofluorocarbons. This greenhouse gas was not only potent, but is also reactive to free oxygen atoms on the ozone molecule (Andersen, 2002). This would create two different sources where ozone is being broken down, while still only having one source of ozone being re-created!

Antarctic Ozone Hole

Image from Wikimedia Commons.

You may be wondering how or why the Ozone Hole builds in the Antarctic since that region is far from the source of greenhouse gas emissions. However, our atmosphere mixes and distributes air through wind and convection (Noaa.gov, 2010). Not only that, but due to the polar region's cold temperatures, Polar stratospheric clouds (PSCs) are created by freezing nitric acid and water, creating clouds of ice particles (Jabbar, 1993). This in turn increases the presence of active chlorine atoms that are responsible for breaking down ozone.

Only the Beginning

The Ozone Hole, as shown in the above picture, seemed to be contained in the Antarctic region. However, it has been found that ozone depletion has expanded to the middle and high latitudes of both hemispheres, covering a lot of the Americas, Europe, Australia, and more (Jabbar, 1993). This is quite detrimental for our Earth, because without ozone, we cannot be protected from the harmful ultraviolet rays from the sun!

The Ozone hole has expanded on average over time throughout the years. This has to do with the amount of greenhouse gases that are present in our atmosphere. Each greenhouse gas has a different level of effectiveness in reducing our ozone layer.

As previously stated, the Ozone Hole in Antarctica became larger and more prominent after the large use of CFCs as refrigerants (and hair spray). We are able to see from this table that CFCs do have a really large Global Warming Potential! Luckily, we have had a global plan to help mitigate this issue and prevent the use of CFCs completely. Even then, simply by looking at the concentrations in PPB, we can see that Carbon Dioxide exists in really high concentrations, and it also wasn't always this high!

CO2 Levels Over Time

Data from NOAA.gov.

CO2 levels have been quite constant over time throughout history, with variations that seemed to repeat. However, as shown in the graph above, this dramatically shifted near the 1950s, and now doesn't reach concentrations below 300 ppm anymore! As of October 2020, the CO2 concentration is 415 ppm. If nothing is done to reduce our emissions, they will only get higher, and will be more detrimental for our ozone layer!

Why is This Important?

The Ozone Layer is responsible for preventing excess ultraviolet rays from penetrating the atmosphere, which allows life on Earth. Greenhouse gases, on the other hand, not only prevent the ozone from blocking these rays, but also prevents heat from escaping the Earth and traps it inside. This, if not dealt with, will cause the Earth to heat up and damage different parts of the Earth's systems! These include sea level rise (since ice will melt) and increased ocean acidity from dissolved CO2 and temperature which can cause mass extinctions in both the ocean and land! Plants are natural CO2 mitigators, but the amount of plants that we have cannot keep up with our emissions, so it must be reduced before our planet reaches a point where it cannot be saved.

A Fix Throughout History?

After discovering the vast effects that CFCs had on our atmospheric ozone layer, people knew something had to be done. In April 1977, a world plan was created to show the effects of the extra ultraviolet radiation had on animals and plants, along with steps and recommendations to take to prevent ozone depletion (Andersen, 2002). Shortly after that came the Montreal Protocol, a treaty that was made to prevent the creation of CFCs and other really impactful greenhouse gases (Andersen, 2002). This did not include carbon or methane emissions that are still widely used today.

The Paris Agreement

A most recent treaty to combat greenhouse gas emissions is the Paris Agreement. The purpose of this agreement is to increase our responses to the issue of climate change caused by greenhouse gases and keep a temperature rise below 2 degrees Celcius this century (Unfccc.int, 2020). This agreement provides countries an easier way to keep below this threshold and prevent massive damage to our Earth's ecosystem. Countries are meant to combat climate change, but the Paris Agreement makes it easier for countries to adjust to climate change mitigation strategies at their own pace. Nevertheless, there are still some issues.

The United States

The 45th President of the United States, Donald Trump, believed that climate change was not happening, and greenhouse gas emissions were unreal. On November 4th, 2019, the United States withdrew from the Paris Agreement which became in effect on November 4th, 2020. This meant that the U.S. wasn't bound to change their emissions to prevent our world from overheating and dying. However, the 46th President-elect, Joe Biden, has stated that he will rejoin the Paris Agreement when he is in office. The Paris Agreement is something all countries must be part of, or efforts would've been for nothing.

What Can You Do?

The first thing to do to prevent climate change is to understand that there's a problem, which you've already done! Without acknowledging climate change, people wouldn't realize there would be something that needs fixing.

As mentioned throughout this site, a lot of greenhouse gas emissions come from carbon dioxide, which in turn comes from coal, oil, and other gases that are used for fuel and electricity. By utilizing green energy or an electric car, you are reducing your own carbon footprint which would reduce emissions as well. If everyone did this, our greenhouse gas emissions would be at an all-time low. Another source of greenhouse gas emissions is methane from agriculture, which is quite high in certain livestock and farms! By eating alternative proteins (or at least not beef!) you can also immensely reduce emissions.

Another way to reduce emissions is to, well, tell others! Awareness on this subject is important, and not a lot of people know its true effects. I guess that's what happens when scientific fact becomes controversial, even though its finalized, accurate data. Spreading the word allows more people to take action and increase the chances of full mitigation becoming reality.
